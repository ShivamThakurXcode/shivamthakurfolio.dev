Headless
e-commerce storefront

A rebuild that turned a slow, monolithic shop into a fast, headless storefront — statically generated, Stripe-powered, and measurably better at converting.

Client

Retail brand

Year

2025

Role

Full Stack Developer

Timeline

2 months

Overview

A retail brand’s store was slow and hard to change. I rebuilt the storefront as a headless Next.js app — decoupled from the commerce backend, statically generated, and wired to Stripe for checkout.

The problem

The legacy platform coupled content, catalog and presentation together. Pages were heavy, loaded in several seconds, and every design tweak meant fighting the theme engine. Mobile conversion was suffering.

The solution

I decoupled the front end: product content flows from a headless CMS and commerce API, and Next.js pre-renders every product page as static HTML with incremental regeneration for updates. Images are optimized and lazy-loaded, the cart lives client-side, and Stripe Checkout handles payment securely without touching our servers.

A store that loads in under a second converts better — performance is a feature, not an afterthought.

FAQ

What does headless e-commerce mean here?

The storefront front end is fully decoupled from the commerce backend. Product content comes from a headless CMS and commerce API, and the Next.js front end renders it as static pages. This gives full control over the UI and performance while the backend handles catalog, inventory and orders.

How was checkout handled?

With Stripe. The cart is managed client-side and hands off to Stripe Checkout for payment, which keeps sensitive card handling off our servers and gives a secure, conversion-optimized payment flow out of the box.

Why did conversion improve after the rebuild?

Speed and clarity. Statically generated product pages load in under a second, images are optimized and lazy-loaded, and a streamlined checkout removed friction. Faster, cleaner pages consistently convert better, and here it was about a 25% lift.
