mydigisence

A SaaS platform that gives any business a complete digital presence and a services marketplace — a website, listings and bookings, all in one place.

Overview

mydigisence helps businesses go from “no online presence” to a working digital storefront in one sitting. It combines a website and profile, a searchable services marketplace, and bookings — so a business can get discovered and start taking work without wiring together half a dozen separate tools.

The problem

Small businesses and independent providers were juggling a website builder, a separate listing site, a booking tool and a payments app — none of which talked to each other. Setup was slow, data was scattered, and there was no single place to manage a digital presence.

The solution

I built mydigisence as a multi-tenant SaaS platform. A Next.js front end renders fast, SEO-friendly public pages and a rich dashboard. A Node.js API sits over two data stores used deliberately: PostgreSQL for transactional data — accounts, listings, bookings, payments — where relationships and consistency matter, and MongoDB for flexible content like storefront pages, profiles and media. The result is one cohesive product where presence, marketplace and bookings share the same account.

One account, one dashboard — a business gets a site, a marketplace listing and bookings without touching five different tools.

FAQ

What is mydigisence?

mydigisence is a SaaS platform that gives businesses a complete digital presence and a services marketplace in one place — a website and profile, service listings, and bookings — so a business can get online and start taking work without stitching together separate tools.

What technology stack powers mydigisence?

A Next.js front end for fast, SEO-friendly pages, a Node.js API, PostgreSQL for relational data like accounts, listings and bookings, and MongoDB for flexible content such as profiles and media. This mix keeps structured data safe while staying flexible where the content varies.

Why use both PostgreSQL and MongoDB?

Transactional data — users, orders, bookings — belongs in PostgreSQL where relationships and consistency matter. Rich, evolving content like storefront pages and media metadata fits MongoDB's flexible documents. Using each where it is strongest keeps the platform both reliable and adaptable.
